# TBOT Full Audit — Veteran Trader × Senior Quant Review
**Date:** 2026-06-19  ·  **Scope:** strategies, aggregators, veteran trade manager, SL/TP, AI validation, risk
**Lens:** what actually makes money vs. what is academically tidy. Cross-validated against how real systematic desks build multi-strategy engines.

---

## 1. Verdict up front

The engineering quality here is genuinely high — clean separation of concerns, config-driven, defensive coding, real microstructure awareness (stale-price veto, flash veto, session blocks, economic calendar). This is well above typical retail bot code.

But the system has **one structural problem that caps profitability and three that bleed it**, and they are not bugs in the usual sense — they are design decisions that fight each other:

1. **The MRS is silent because the architecture is designed to silence it.** It is not a tuning accident; the strategy's own gates plus four downstream veto layers make Mode 1 and Mode 2 statistically near-impossible to fire. (Section 3, with log evidence.)
2. **Trend-following and mean-reversion are averaged together in a weighted sum.** These are opposite edges. Summing them means a correct MR signal is routinely cancelled or penalized by the trend voters. Institutions *regime-switch* between these; they don't average them. (Section 2.)
3. **Signal generation is over-vetoed end to end.** A signal must survive the strategy gate → Livermore hard veto → silent-zone gate → gatekeeper regime penalty → consensus threshold → AI validator (~80% reject in the live log) → profit/vol/sniper filters. The expected number of survivors is very low, and you cannot earn an edge you never express. (Section 5.)
4. **There is a live risk-cap bug:** `max_total_open_risk = 45` is consumed as a fraction (4500% of equity), so the aggregate portfolio risk cap is effectively **off**. (Section 8.)

None of this means "throw it away." The components are good. The *composition* is wrong. Below is the honest, specific breakdown.

## 2. The core architectural flaw: you cannot average a trend follower and a mean reverter

`PerformanceWeightedAggregator._calculate_score()` (signal_aggregator.py:2222) builds a single scalar:

```
score = Σ (strategy_conf × weight)  + agreement_bonus ± regime_adj
        − Σ (opposing_conf × opposition_penalty × weight)
```

Mean reversion and trend following are, by construction, *anti-correlated signal generators*. MR fires when price is stretched and likely to snap back; TF fires when price is extending and likely to continue. When MR is right (fade the extension), TF is usually firing the other way — so MR's contribution is met with a `*_oppose` penalty (lines 2256-2262) and the agreement bonus never triggers. The math systematically suppresses whichever strategy is currently correct for the regime.

This is the root reason MR "misses most mean-reversion opportunities" even on the rare occasions it does fire: even a high-conviction MR signal has to overcome two trend voters (TF + EMA, weights 0.5/0.4) plus the regime tilt. With MR's own confidence capped around 0.60–0.75 (mean_reversion.py:528-530, 648-649) and the independent-override bar at **0.75** (signal_aggregator.py:272), MR essentially never wins a vote or fires alone.

**How desks actually do this.** Multi-strategy books do not vote trend and reversion into one number. They run a **regime classifier** (you already have one — MTF + Livermore) and then *route capital to the strategy whose edge matches the regime*: trend sleeve gets weight in trending/expansion regimes, reversion sleeve gets weight in range/mean-reverting regimes, and the sleeves are sized by their own recent risk-adjusted performance (the "performance-weighted" idea, applied at the **sleeve** level, not blended per-bar). The two sleeves should be allowed to be in the market at the same time on *different* assets, but should rarely be netted against each other on the *same* asset/bar.

The good news: the **Council aggregator already moves in this direction** — it treats reversion as one judge among six bidirectional judges with a regime-gated required-score (council_aggregator.py:880+, `_judge_reversion_bidirectional` at 2851). The Council's reversion logic is also far more likely to fire than the standalone MRS (simple, classic: RSI crosses back over 30 + close > prior high). The problem is the active mode is `hybrid` (config `aggregator_settings.mode`), and in performance/hybrid-performance paths the three-mode MRS is the MR voice — and it is muzzled.

## 3. Why the Mean Reversion engine is silent — quantified

The MRS (mean_reversion.py) was rebuilt into a Livermore-state-routed three-mode machine. The intent is sound (don't fade blindly), but each mode stacks so many *mandatory* gates that the firing probability collapses. Then the aggregator vetoes what little survives.

### 3a. The strategy-internal funnel

**Mode 1 — Pullback Completion** (NATURAL_RETRACEMENT, LONG only). To emit a signal it needs **all** of:
- not vetoed by `vol_down_ratio` (line 465),
- **AND** a BB/KC squeeze *or* NR7 compression active (line 482) — hard mandatory,
- **AND** a Wyckoff spring detected: wick sweeps prior swing low, closes back above, penetration 0.5–5%, *and* current-bar volume < spring-bar volume (lines 250-268) — hard mandatory,
- **AND** ≥2 of 4 optional confluences (line 502).

Requiring *volatility compression* **and** *a textbook spring* **and** *2/4 confluences* simultaneously is a multi-condition AND chain where each link is individually uncommon. The joint probability per bar is tiny.

**Mode 2 — Counter-Trend** (SECONDARY states). Requires **4 of 4** optional conditions (line 589) — vol_contraction AND hidden_divergence AND bb_contraction AND ma_proximity, all at once — plus ADX<25, plus "BB closed back inside," plus (BTC) a z-score gate. Demanding *all four* heterogeneous confluences on the same bar is statistically close to never. This mode is effectively dead code.

**Mode 3 — Climax Fade** (MAIN states). leg_stretch >1.5× AND >2.5×ATR from anchor AND a high-rank reversal candle AND above-average volume — all mandatory.

### 3b. The downstream veto stack (signal_aggregator.py:3095-3169)

Even a surviving MR signal hits the **Livermore Hard Veto** layer:
- Block A: any LONG zeroed in NATURAL_REBOUND.
- Block C: MR SHORT zeroed in NATURAL_RETRACEMENT; MR LONG zeroed in NATURAL_REBOUND.
- Plus the **silent-zone gate** (3303-3312): if the 4H Livermore state is NATURAL, **all** signals (MR/TF/EMA) are zeroed.

So MR is allowed to act only in a narrow set of states, and Mode 1 (its highest-quality setup) lives in NATURAL_RETRACEMENT, which is *also* the state most likely to be inside a 4H silent zone that zeroes everything.

### 3c. The evidence (live log, bot_20260619.log, decoded)

State routing actually observed:
```
lsm_1h=MAIN_DOWN       31   → Mode 3 (climax fade LONG)
lsm_1h=NATURAL_REBOUND 14   → MR SILENT by design (returns 0)
lsm_1h=MAIN_UP         14   → Mode 3 (climax fade SHORT)
NATURAL_RETRACEMENT     0   → Mode 1 never even reached
SECONDARY_*             0   → Mode 2 never even reached
MR fires: 12   MR zeros: 33
```
Every single MR signal that fired in that window was **Mode 3 on GBPAUD** (climax fade). Mode 1 and Mode 2 — the *core* mean-reversion edges — did not fire once because the bot was never in their routing state during the window, and even when it is, the gates are too tight. This is exactly the "MRS mostly silent, missing most reversion opportunities" you observed, and it is reproducible from the logs, not a feeling.

### 3d. Fixes (in priority order)
1. **Stop netting MR against TF on the same bar** (Section 2). Biggest lever.
2. **Relax Mode 2 from 4/4 → 2/3 of meaningful confluences**, and drop the weakest of the four (ma_proximity is the noisiest). 4/4 is not "selective," it's "off."
3. **Decouple Mode 1 from the compression gate OR the spring**, not both. Require spring + 2/4, and treat compression as a *confidence booster*, not a hard gate.
4. **Let Mode 1 also reach NATURAL states without being zeroed by the 4H silent-zone gate** — that gate currently eats the very setup Mode 1 was built for (the code comment at 3300 even admits Mode 1 "will add re-entry logic that bypasses this hold," but the bypass isn't wired).
5. Raise MR's confidence ceiling or lower its independent-override threshold (0.75 → ~0.62) so a clean reversion can fire on its own.

## 4. Trend Following engine

This is the healthiest engine in the system. `_generate_live_signal` (trend_following.py:399) scores MA alignment, MACD, DI, price position, a **slope-aware ADX bonus** (good — distinguishes a rising ADX=26 from a rolling-over ADX=40, lines 445-492), a squeeze bonus, and 4H context. Threshold 2.5, normalization 6.5. Honest, standard, defensible.

Concerns:
- **4H counter-penalty of 2.0** (config `h4_counter_penalty`) is heavy and is applied *both* to add to the aligned side and subtract from the counter side — effectively a 3.5-point swing. Combined with the Livermore hard vetoes and the gatekeeper regime block, counter-trend trend-signals are triple-punished. That's defensible for a trend follower, but it means TF is almost purely a with-trend continuation engine — which is fine *if* the reversion sleeve is actually allowed to cover the other regime. Right now it isn't (Section 2/3), so the book is structurally long-trend-only.
- The squeeze bonus adds **+1.0 to both** bull and bear simultaneously (line 500-501). In a squeeze this can push *both* sides over the 2.5 threshold; the tie-break is just `bull > bear`. Fine, but it means a squeeze inflates confidence on whichever side barely wins — a low-quality reason to size up. Consider making squeeze a confidence multiplier gated on breakout direction, not a flat both-sides add.
- Confidence is `score/6.5` — a structural signal that scores 2.6 emits confidence 0.40, often below the various `min_confidence_to_use` and AI bypass bars. Many valid TF signals die on confidence rounding.

Overall: keep it. It's the part that's working.

## 5. The cumulative veto problem (the real profitability killer)

Walk a signal through the live path and multiply survival probabilities:

| Stage | Effect |
|---|---|
| Strategy gate (esp. MR) | low base fire rate |
| Livermore Hard Veto A–D | zeroes counter-structure entries |
| 4H silent-zone gate | zeroes **everything** in NATURAL 4H |
| Gatekeeper regime block/penalty | hard-blocks or scales counter-trend |
| Consensus threshold + opposition penalty | nets strategies against each other |
| AI validator | **~80% reject in the live log** (15+12+4 block/reject vs 8 pass) |
| Vol / profit / sniper / ATR-expansion filters | further attrition |
| NY-open + econ-calendar + flash + stale | time/space blackouts |

Each layer is individually reasonable. **Stacked, they're multiplicative**, and the system's biggest risk is not a bad trade — it's *no trades, or only the few that survive a gauntlet so narrow the sample is too small to express any statistical edge*. A desk would call this "over-fitting the filters to the last drawdown." Every one of these blocks looks like it was added in response to a specific loss (the comments confirm it: "Gold frozen 47.6h," "NY open 53% WR," "the exact losing pattern in the MRS"). That is recency-driven defensive accretion, and it compounds into paralysis.

**Recommendation:** instrument a *funnel report* — for each asset, count signals generated → survived each gate → executed, per day. You almost certainly cannot see right now how many real opportunities each veto is killing. Until that's visible, every "add another safety gate" decision is blind. (This is cheap: you already log every block reason.)

The AI validator deserves its own scrutiny: an 80% reject rate means the ML layer is either (a) genuinely filtering garbage, or (b) the dominant P&L driver masquerading as a "filter." There's a circuit breaker for exactly this (`_check_ai_circuit_breaker`, ai_bypass_threshold 0.85) — good — but you should be A/B logging "would-have-traded" outcomes for AI-rejected signals to know whether the validator adds or destroys edge. Right now it's an unfalsifiable veto.

## 6. SL/TP and the Veteran Trade Manager

The VTM is the most institutionally-literate file in the repo. Strengths:
- ATR-based stops with **regime-adaptive multipliers** (wider in bear/high-vol, floors for reversion), MA-shield tuck-behind, structural-stop override, spread floor, min_sl_pct floor (veteran_trade_manager.py:713-880). This is real risk craftsmanship.
- **Weighted R:R validation across all partials** (lines 250-268) — correctly avoids the common bug of judging a partial-exit system by TP1 alone.
- ADX-conditioned targets (shrink in chop, extend in momentum), TP de-duplication, MFE/MAE logging, runner promotion.

Honest concerns:

1. **The REVERSION SL/TP path is much weaker than the trend path** (lines 732-752). It uses a single TP at `ema_4h_50` and a wick-based stop of only `last_bar_low − 0.5×ATR`. That's a *tight* stop with **no partial ladder and no R:R pre-flight check** (the `validate_trade_setup` min-RR logic is the TREND path). For a mean-reversion entry sitting inside a range, a 0.5×ATR wick stop will get swept constantly — and there's no minimum-R:R guard ensuring the EMA target is far enough away to be worth the risk. If/when you revive the MR sleeve, this path needs the same R:R discipline as TREND, or your reversion win-rate will be eaten by stop-outs. The `atr_multiplier` floor of 2.0 for REVERSION (line 346) is computed but **not used** in the reversion branch of `_calculate_initial_levels` — the branch hardcodes `0.5×ATR`. That looks like a real inconsistency.

2. **TP1 at 1.0R–1.5R with 45% off** is aggressive de-risking. It locks a high hit-rate but caps the right tail; your expectancy then depends entirely on the 25% runner. With a trend follower that's defensible; just know the system's profitability is structurally **runner-dependent**, so anything that prematurely kills runners (tight trailing, breakeven-too-early) directly attacks the only high-R component. Audit `runner_trail_atr_multiplier = 1.0` — a 1×ATR trail on the runner is tight and will clip trends early. Institutions trailing a trend runner typically use 2.5–3.5×ATR or a structure trail.

3. **Breakeven-after-bars + early-lock at 0.5×ATR** (config `early_lock_atr_multiplier: 0.5`) moves stops to BE very quickly. Combined with the 1×ATR runner trail, the system is biased toward *small wins and scratches*. That produces a smooth equity curve and a mediocre profit factor. If the goal is to make money (not to minimize drawdown), loosen the runner.

## 7. EMA strategy

Standard third voter (ema_strategy.py): MA-stack scoring, 4H context, fixed confidence 0.75 on the clean-cross path (lines 518-525). It largely duplicates TrendFollowing's directional view, which is why it correlates with TF and adds to the "two trend voters gang up on MR" problem. In a regime-switched design it should be folded into the trend sleeve, not counted as an independent third vote — three voters where two are trend-followers guarantees a trend-biased book.

This part is professional.

## 9. How this compares to institutional multi-strategy engines

Where TBOT matches the pros: ATR/vol-scaled stops, partial-exit laddering with weighted R:R, regime detection, microstructure circuit breakers, per-asset parameterization, dollar-risk budgeting, continuous retraining pipeline.

Where it diverges from how money is actually made:
- **Pros allocate across uncorrelated sleeves; TBOT averages correlated signals into one number.** Trend and reversion should be two books, sized by regime and rolling Sharpe, not netted per bar.
- **Pros measure and protect opportunity capture.** TBOT measures only what it blocked, never what blocking cost. The funnel is invisible.
- **Pros let winners run.** TBOT's fast BE + 1×ATR runner trail + 45%-off-at-1R is optimized for hit-rate and smoothness, which usually means a profit factor near 1. A trend system lives or dies on the runner.
- **Pros treat an 80%-reject ML filter as a strategy and validate its marginal P&L.** TBOT treats it as a safety gate and never checks whether it helps.

# ADDENDUM — 2026-06-20 (round 2: additional flaws + execution/data layer)

## 12. Additional flaws found after the first pass (all verified)

**12.1 Trade-manager state is rebuilt from scratch on restart, not restored.** When a VTM is missing for an open position, `main.py:3444` constructs a *fresh* `VeteranTradeManager` from the original entry price but **current** OHLC — so `_calculate_initial_levels` recomputes a brand-new SL/TP on today's ATR. Partial TPs already taken, breakeven moves, runner activation, and MFE/MAE are lost; a deeply-trailed stop can snap back out to the initial ATR distance and hand back locked profit. The VTM *has* `to_dict`/`from_dict` (lines 2153/2186), but this recovery path doesn't use them. Restarts happen (`restart.bat`, `run_bot.ps1`). **Fix:** persist VTM state on every mutation and restore via `from_dict` on reattach.

**12.2 ML models are 5+ weeks stale despite autotrain being ON.** Every strategy `.pkl` is dated May 5–14; today is June 20. Both `phase_config.autotrainer_enabled` and `ml.enable_autotrain` read **True**, yet no fresh artifacts exist — the "7-day continuous learning" pipeline is enabled but not producing/promoting models. It's silently failing or no-op'ing. **Fix:** verify the retrainer actually runs end-to-end and promotes; add an alert if model age > N days.

**12.3 The "AI sniper" is dead code still listed in the architecture.** `_check_sniper_filter` returns `True` unconditionally (signal_aggregator.py:2472). What CLAUDE.md/README call "AI validation (OHLCSniper + PatternMiner)" is now really S/R-proximity + candlestick heuristics, much in `fallback_mode`. The ~80% reject rate is therefore rule-based gating, not a trained edge model — easier to mis-tune into paralysis, harder to justify. **Fix:** either re-wire a validated model or stop calling it "AI" and validate the heuristic filter's marginal P&L.

**12.4 The two aggregators carry duplicated safety logic that has drifted.** Stale threshold **65 min** (performance, signal_aggregator.py:301) vs **30 min** (council, council_aggregator.py:148). NY-open block lists differ (council adds US100/NAS100/XAUUSD/OIL aliases). Econ-calendar block has a "block-all if no currency listed" fallback in performance but not council. In `hybrid` mode you cannot predict which rule set is live, and a fix to one file silently won't apply to the other. **Fix:** extract shared pre-trade gates into one module both aggregators call.

**12.5 Documentation drift.** CLAUDE.md still lists the disconnected sniper, fixed 45/30/25 partials, and a circuit-breaker spec that per-asset config no longer matches. Anyone reasoning from the docs starts wrong.

## 12B. The continuous-learning loop is disconnected from live trading (found 2026-06-20)

Triggered by the question "does the autotrainer feed the performance aggregator?" — the answer is **no**, on two compounding levels:

**12B.1 Hot-swap is a silent no-op (both aggregators).** `autotrainer._reload_aggregator` guarded on `hasattr(aggregator, "mr_strategy"/"tf_strategy"/"ema_strategy")`, but both `PerformanceWeightedAggregator` and `InstitutionalCouncilAggregator` store their strategies as `s_mean_reversion` / `s_trend_following` / `s_ema`. So every hot-swap did nothing; retrained models never reached the live aggregators (only a full restart reloads from disk). **Fixed** (Phase 6.1) to use the correct attribute names + a `load_model` guard.

**12B.2 The strategies don't use the trained models at inference (the deeper issue).** `BaseStrategy` has a real RandomForest `generate_signal` (`self.model.predict_proba`), but all three concrete strategies **override** `generate_signal` with rule-based logic (MR = Livermore three-mode; TF/EMA = scoring) and never reference `self.model`. The `.pkl` models are loaded into `self.model` at startup (`load_models()`) and then ignored. So the autotrainer's RandomForest models are **decorative for live signal generation** — retraining changes nothing live, on either aggregator, even after the 12B.1 fix.

**12B.3 The models look leaky anyway.** `training_metadata.json` shows mean-reversion CV accuracy ≈ **0.988** across assets — implausibly high for a 3-class signal label built from future returns. Strong smell of label leakage / look-ahead. It is *fortunate* these aren't driving trades; wiring them in as-is would likely hurt.

**Net:** "continuous learning" currently trains and evaluates models that never influence a live trade. Decide explicitly: either (a) wire `self.model` into the strategies' signal path (a Phase-3+ design change requiring leakage-free relabelling + heavy walk-forward backtests), or (b) accept the engines are rule-based and reclassify the ML pipeline as research-only (and stop calling it the live "brain"). The Phase 6.1 fixes make the retrainer *run and report*; they intentionally do **not** change what drives live signals.

## 13. Execution & data-layer audit

This layer is where intent becomes real fills, and it has the highest-severity *operational* bugs in the system.

**13.1 [HIGH — BTC/Binance] Position registered at the requested price, not the fill price.** `binance_handler.py` computes `executed_price` and `slippage` (lines 1201-1231) then registers the position with `entry_price=current_price` anyway (line 1284). The VTM's stop distance, targets, R:R, and all P&L are anchored to the *pre-trade* price, so on any slippage the real risk and reported P&L are wrong, and the slippage tracking is purely cosmetic. **MT5 does this correctly** (`entry_price=execution_price`, mt5_handler.py:861) — so this is an inconsistent, fixable bug on the crypto side. Use `executed_price`.

**13.2 [MED-HIGH — all assets] Sizing stop ≠ executed stop, so realized risk exceeds budget.** Position size is computed with `atr_multiplier` taken straight from config (binance:986 ≈1.5, mt5:522), but the VTM then places the *actual* stop with regime-adaptive multipliers and floors of **2.0–2.5×ATR** (veteran_trade_manager.py:342-355). Since `size = risk$ / stop_distance`, a wider real stop than the sizing stop means every trade risks more than the intended `risk_pct` — roughly 30–60% over budget in adverse/reversion regimes. **Fix:** size against the *same* stop the VTM will actually use (compute VTM levels first, then size).

**13.3 [MED — all assets] Order retries are not idempotent → double-fill risk.** Both handlers retry market orders up to 2× (binance:1140, mt5:1184) with no `clientOrderId`/dedup token. If an order fills but the response errors or times out, the retry re-sends and you get 2× the intended position. The duplicate-side guard (binance:926) checks the bot's portfolio state, not in-flight exchange state, so it won't catch a within-loop double-send. **Fix:** attach a deterministic `newClientOrderId`/`comment` token and check exchange state before re-sending.

**13.4 [MED — MT5/Gold/FX/indices] Broker stop is best-effort, not guaranteed.** MT5 entries are sent with `sl: 0.0` (mt5_handler.py:1173) and the stop is pushed *after* the fill (line 924), only if the config flag is on (it is) **and** the VTM was created; failure is swallowed with a warning, no retry, no abort (line 930). So there's a real window between fill and a *successful* SL push, and indefinite naked exposure if the push fails or the VTM wasn't built (e.g. the earlier OHLC fetch failed). On leveraged FX/Gold over a news spike or weekend gap this is the scenario that blows an account. Futures/BTC is safer — the STOP_MARKET is placed atomically inside the futures open. **Fix:** verify SL-push retcode; on failure, retry then emergency-close if it still fails. Consider sending the SL *with* the entry where the broker allows.

**13.5 Data manager — mostly clean.** UTC normalization (tz_localize/tz_convert), duplicate removal, sort_index, and klines retry are all correct — better than most retail code. Two notes: (a) execution can run on testnet while data is always fetched live; currently `api.binance.testnet=False` so it's fine, but a config flip would silently make BTC fills fake against live signals — add a startup banner/assert. (b) `clean_data` uses `ffill()` which can synthesize flat candles across gaps; fine for training, but never feed ffilled data to live signals — the stale-price veto is your only backstop.

## 11. One-paragraph honest summary

You've built a very competently engineered, risk-aware, microstructure-savvy execution framework with a genuinely good trade manager and a professional risk layer — and then composed the alpha layer in a way that cancels its own mean-reversion edge and buries every signal under a multiplicative stack of recency-driven vetoes. The MRS isn't broken; it's been gated into silence and then out-voted by two trend engines on the rare bar it survives. Fix the risk-cap bug today, make the signal funnel visible, stop averaging trend against reversion, and loosen the runner. Do those four things and you move from "a beautifully defended machine that rarely trades and scratches when it does" to "a system that actually expresses an edge." The craftsmanship to win is already in the repo; it's pointed slightly at the wrong target.
